§05

The 3 Questions That Trapped Everyone

These weren't hard questions. They were familiar-looking questions with hidden wrong turns. 65–80% of students made the mistake in each one.

Trap #1 · Gravitation
Work done to raise mass m to height equal to Earth's radius R
What 80% of students did
Used W = mgh = mgR. Simple substitution. Wrong answer. The formula W=mgh only works for small heights where g is constant.
Correct thinking
ΔPE = −GMm/2R − (−GMm/R) = GMm/2R = mgR/2. Answer is mgR/2, not mgR. Height equals R — g is not constant here.
Trap #2 · Motion in Straight Line
Ball thrown vertically upward. Find position after 6 seconds. Answer came negative.
What 70% of students did
Got a negative displacement. Assumed calculation error. Re-calculated 3 times. Panicked. Left it blank or marked wrong option.
Correct thinking
Negative displacement = ball is below the point of projection. Ball went up, came back, and continued below (e.g. thrown from a building). Formula was correct. Interpretation was the test.
Trap #3 · Current Electricity
Metre bridge: cell and galvanometer positions are interchanged. What happens?
What 65% of students did
Thought the balance point would shift completely or the galvanometer would only deflect one side. Marked wrong option.
Correct thinking
The balance point remains the same. At balance, galvanometer reads zero regardless of which side it's on. Only deflection pattern near balance changes — both sides deflect, with zero at balance point. This is pure concept, zero calculation.

Topper's Checkpoint

Five things the highest scorers understood about NEET 2026 Physics that average students missed entirely.

  1. 1
    They attempted easy questions in under 30 seconds
    12 easy questions × 30 seconds = 6 minutes saved. That's 6 extra minutes for the hard questions. Toppers don't try to be careful on easy questions — they're fast and move on. Average students spend 90 seconds on a question worth the same 4 marks as a hard one.
  2. 2
    They left the multi-diode circuit and attempted it last
    The Semiconductor multi-diode question (Q3) was the hardest question in the section. Toppers recognized it within 10 seconds, marked it to revisit, and moved on to collect easier marks. Average students spent 4+ minutes on it early and disrupted their momentum.
  3. 3
    They read "what is asked" before "what is given"
    The Gravitation trap (W = mgR/2) and the V-T graph trap both came from not reading what was asked carefully. Toppers read the question ending — "work done", "displacement", "position" — first. Then they read the setup. This one habit saves 3–5 wrong answers per paper.
  4. 4
    They treated matching questions as 4-mark gifts
    4 matching questions in this paper. Each takes under 60 seconds if your facts are organized. Toppers had matching flashcards for elastic moduli, EM wave production, and quantum concepts. Average students spent 3 minutes guessing. Matching questions are free marks — if you prepared them.
  5. 5
    They never ignored absent chapters
    Motion in a Plane was absent in 2026. Toppers preparing for 2027 will not reduce it in their revision — they know the paper rotates. The students who drop absent chapters are exactly who the paper is designed to punish in the next year.

The 67-Second Challenge

This question appeared in NEET 2026 (Medium difficulty). You have 67 seconds. Start when you click the button.

Can You Beat the Clock?
NEET 2026 · Kinetic Theory · Medium
Question
A flask contains argon and chlorine in the ratio of 2:1 by mass. The temperature of the mixture is 27°C. The ratio of root mean square speed of the molecules, vrms(Ar) / vrms(Cl₂) is:

(Atomic mass of Ar = 40u, Molecular mass of Cl₂ = 70u)
67
vrms = √(3RT/M). The mass ratio (2:1) is a distractor — it does not affect vrms. Only molecular mass M matters.

vrms(Ar)/vrms(Cl₂) = √(MCl₂/MAr) = √(70/40) = √(7/4).

The trap: Students tried to use the mass ratio 2:1 in the calculation. It is completely irrelevant to vrms.
